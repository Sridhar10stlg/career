# 05 — Consulting Roadmap

> Goal: become a technologist who can **speak business, understand stakeholders, drive decisions, build cases, and influence transformation.** This is your latest-maturing track (Year 2–3 focus) — but the *habits* start now, in how you communicate.
> Core truth: **Consulting = structured thinking + earned trust + clear communication.** Technical skill gets you in the room; these keep you valuable.

---

## The consulting mindset

Internalize these shifts:

1. **From "how" to "why."** Engineers jump to solutions. Consultants first ask *why* — what's the real problem, whose problem, what's it worth solving.
2. **Outcomes over outputs.** Nobody buys a pipeline; they buy "faster decisions / lower cost / less risk." Always translate tech → business outcome.
3. **Hypothesis-first.** Don't boil the ocean. Form a hypothesis, then seek the data that confirms/kills it (see [07](07-thinking-frameworks.md)).
4. **Be the trusted advisor, not the order-taker.** Push back well; recommend, don't just execute. (*The Trusted Advisor*, Maister.)
5. **Communicate top-down.** Answer first, then support (Pyramid Principle). Execs don't want your journey; they want your conclusion.
6. **Manage the relationship, not just the task.** Trust = (credibility + reliability + intimacy) / self-orientation. Lower your self-orientation; it's the biggest lever.

## 1. Requirement gathering

**Skill:** extract what stakeholders *actually* need (which is rarely what they first say).

- Distinguish needs vs wants vs constraints.
- Functional vs non-functional requirements (the NFRs architects live in).
- Capture assumptions explicitly; surface the unspoken.

**Techniques:** structured interviews, "5 Whys," job-to-be-done framing, current-state vs desired-state mapping.

**Exercise:** Next requirement at work, write a one-page requirements doc: *business goal → users → functional reqs → NFRs → constraints → assumptions → open questions.* Review it with the requester. Notice how much you initially misunderstood.

---

## 2. Stakeholder management

**Skill:** identify, understand, and align the people who matter.

- **Stakeholder map:** who's impacted, their interests, their influence (power/interest grid).
- Tailor message to audience: exec (outcomes, $, risk) vs engineer (design, trade-offs) vs end-user (workflow).
- Manage expectations; communicate proactively; never let a stakeholder be surprised.

**Exercise:** For your current project, draw a power/interest grid of everyone involved. For each high-power person, write one sentence: "What do they care about most, and how does my work serve it?"

---

## 3. Discovery workshops

**Skill:** run a structured session that turns vague business pain into a shaped problem + direction.

**Structure of a good discovery:**
1. Frame the problem & goals (agree on *the question*).
2. Map current state (process, systems, pain points).
3. Define desired future state & success metrics.
4. Identify gaps, constraints, risks.
5. Diverge on options → converge on a direction.
6. Agree next steps & owners.

**Tools:** whiteboarding, affinity mapping, "How might we..." framing, prioritization (impact/effort).

**Exercise:** Run a mini-discovery (even 30 min) with a colleague on a real problem. Produce a one-page output: problem, current state, desired state, options, recommendation, next steps.

---

## 4. Executive communication

**Skill:** say the important thing first, briefly, in their language.

- **Pyramid Principle:** lead with the answer → group supporting arguments (MECE) → details only if asked.
- **BLUF** (Bottom Line Up Front) in every email/update/deck.
- One slide = one message. Charts make a point, not decorate. (*Storytelling with Data*, Knaflic.)
- Speak in **outcomes, risk, and money** — not tools.

**Exercise:** Take any technical update you wrote recently. Rewrite it in 3 sentences: (1) the conclusion/ask, (2) the why, (3) the next step. Delete everything else. That's exec comms.

---

## 5. Business case & ROI thinking

**Skill:** justify a technical investment in money and risk.

- **Business case skeleton:** problem & cost-of-inaction → proposed solution → benefits (quantified) → costs (build + run) → ROI/payback → risks & mitigations → recommendation.
- **ROI basics:** cost savings, revenue uplift, risk reduction, time-to-value; TCO (total cost of ownership), payback period, NPV (intuition).
- **Quantify the fuzzy:** "saves 10 analyst-hours/week × $rate × 52 = $X/yr" beats "improves efficiency."

**Exercise:** Write a one-page business case for one of your *existing* projects (e.g., the MDM pipeline). What did it cost, what did it save/enable, what's the ROI story? You'll use versions of this in interviews and consulting forever.

---

## 6. Technology advisory

**Skill:** recommend technology decisions defensibly.

- Build-vs-buy analysis; vendor evaluation; weighted decision matrices.
- Map technology choices to business strategy and constraints (budget, skills, timeline, risk appetite).
- Articulate trade-offs in plain language; give a clear recommendation *with* a rationale.

**Exercise:** Snowflake vs Databricks (you know both). Write a one-page advisory: "For a company like [X] with [constraints], I recommend [Y] because [business + technical reasons], accepting [trade-offs]." This is a literal consulting deliverable.

---

## 7. ROI / value framing (the meta-skill)

Always end any technical recommendation with: **"...which means [business outcome]."**
- "We'll add data contracts" → "...which means fewer broken dashboards, so leadership trusts the numbers and decides faster."
- "We'll add an eval harness to the AI agent" → "...which means we can safely ship to customers without reputational risk."

Practice this translation until it's automatic.

## How to practice at Psiog (you're in a services firm — perfect lab)

- Volunteer for any **client-facing** moment: requirement calls, demos, status reviews.
- Offer to write the **proposal / solution-summary** section, not just code.
- Shadow a senior consultant/architect in a client meeting; debrief what they did with stakeholders.
- After every client interaction, journal: *what did they actually care about? what question would have unlocked more?*

## Books
- *The Pyramid Principle* — Barbara Minto (structured communication; the consultant's bible).
- *The Trusted Advisor* — Maister, Green, Galford (relationships & trust).
- *Storytelling with Data* — Cole Nussbaumer Knaflic (visual communication).
- *The McKinsey Way* / *The McKinsey Mind* — Rasiel (problem structuring, MECE, hypothesis-driven).
- *The Software Architect Elevator* — Hohpe (connecting tech to business strategy).
- *Made to Stick* — Heath brothers (making ideas memorable).
